What is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model. It is a type of artificial intelligence trained on vast amounts of text from books, websites, code, and other sources. During training, the model learns statistical patterns in language — how words and phrases tend to follow one another.

At its core, an LLM works by predicting the next token given everything that came before. When you send a prompt, the model generates a response one token at a time, choosing the most likely continuation at each step.

Two widely used real-world examples are GPT-5.4 by OpenAI and Claude Sonnet 4.6 by Anthropic.

What is a Token?

A token is a chunk of text that an LLM reads and writes — not necessarily a whole word. On average, one token is roughly 3–4 characters, or about three-quarters of a word in English.

Tokens matter because they are the unit used to measure context windows (how much text a model can handle in one request) and pricing (providers charge per million input and output tokens).

For example, the sentence "Hello, world!" might be tokenized as: ["Hello", ",", " world", "!"] — four tokens in total.

Tokenization also varies by language. The same idea in Azerbaijani often requires more tokens than in English because subword splits are less efficient for languages with less training data. That means Azerbaijani text can consume more of your context window and cost more per message.
